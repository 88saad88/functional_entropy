FKL · Path-Space Divergence Motivation Method Results
Results

Standard metrics disagree. FKL doesn't.

Six trajectory inference methods, five standard metrics, three synthetic datasets and two real single-cell datasets. The marginal metrics produce inconsistent and often contradictory rankings sometimes reversing their winner when you switch datasets, timepoints, or even just the metric itself. FKL gives one coherent ordering that aligns with the ground truth every time.

1 The benchmark

The paper evaluates six methods spanning the main families of trajectory inference. Three are Schrödinger Bridge (SB) variants, which model trajectories as stochastic processes constrained to match observed marginals. Two are SDE/ODE-based approaches that learn a velocity field directly. Understanding these families matters for interpreting the results.

Method Family Core idea Stochastic?
SBIRR Schrödinger Bridge SB with iterative reference refinement injects structural constraints on the drift to shape implied trajectories Yes
vSB Schrödinger Bridge Vanilla SB baseline with a Brownian motion reference no additional structural constraints Yes
MSBM Schrödinger Bridge Multi-marginal SB matching learns forward/backward diffusion satisfying multiple intermediate marginal constraints simultaneously Yes
MFL SDE Mean-Field Langevin in path space targets drift-diffusion population dynamics by optimising a min-entropy objective relative to Wiener measure Yes
AM SDE Action Matching learns deterministic velocity fields with optional entropic (stochastic) variant used here Opt.
TIGON ODE Formulates TI as dynamic unbalanced optimal transport governed by a PDE, solved via neural ODEs deterministic by design No

Three synthetic datasets where the ground-truth SDE is known and can be simulated exactly: Lotka-Volterra (predator-prey oscillator, 2D), Repressilator (three-gene regulatory oscillator, 3D), and Petal (eight-branch bifurcation mimicking cellular differentiation, 2D). Plus two real scRNA-seq datasets Embryoid Body (EB) and Human Embryonic Stem Cell (hESC) where no ground-truth trajectories exist.

Five marginal metrics evaluated at held-out timepoints: Earth Mover's Distance (W₁/EMD), Wasserstein-2 (W₂), Sliced Wasserstein (SWD), Max-Sliced Wasserstein (MWD), and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD with RBF kernel, bandwidth σ = 1). All five measure how well a method's simulated cells match the observed snapshot distributions they say nothing about how cells moved between snapshots.

2 Marginal metrics are inconsistent

Rankings shown at the last validation snapshot. Switch metrics above to see how the winner changes.

This isn't just a matter of degree the methods that win and lose swap positions entirely depending on which metric you pick. For example, on Lotka-Volterra at the last snapshot, TIGON ranks best under MMD but 5th under W₂. On Repressilator, MFL ranks above SBIRR under MMD but far below it under W₂. No metric gives a consistent picture.

There's also a deeper issue: the same method can rank first at one timepoint and last at another. On Lotka-Volterra, SBIRR and vSB alternate as the top performer across the four validation snapshots. On Repressilator, SBIRR leads early but degrades by the last snapshot while vSB which produces visually implausible dynamics suddenly scores well. A metric that fluctuates this much across timepoints cannot tell you whether a method has captured the underlying process.

Why MMD behaves differently from the Wasserstein metrics

MMD with a fixed bandwidth kernel can saturate once two distributions are further apart than the kernel's bandwidth, the MMD score barely changes even as the actual discrepancy grows. On Repressilator, OT metrics (W₁, W₂) for Action Matching increase sharply across validation snapshots, reflecting trajectories drifting far from the truth. MMD for the same method barely moves. This is not a disagreement about which method is better it's MMD becoming insensitive to exactly the kind of discrepancy that matters.

3 FKL gives a stable, coherent ranking

FKL computes the KL divergence between the full distribution of trajectories produced by each method and the ground-truth path measure. Lower FKL = the method's trajectories are closer, in a distributional sense, to the real thing. VAL (trajectories resampled from the same ground-truth SDE) sets the noise floor the minimum FKL achievable with perfect inference.

Unlike the marginal metrics, FKL's ranking is stable across datasets: it doesn't flip at different timepoints, doesn't change when you compute it in a different direction, and aligns with what you see when you visually inspect the trajectories.

4 A concrete contradiction: Repressilator

The Repressilator is a three-gene oscillator its ground-truth trajectories trace a torus-like path in 3D gene-expression space as the three genes take turns suppressing each other in a cycle. This is genuinely difficult to capture.

Under marginal metrics, TIGON ranks second overall on the Repressilator it passes through the correct regions at every validation snapshot. But look at Figure 1 in the paper: TIGON's trajectories form a smooth spiral that circles the torus monotonically. The real dynamics are stochastic oscillations that repeatedly cycle. TIGON has learned a shortcut that produces the right cross-sections without capturing the oscillatory mechanism.

FKL catches this. TIGON's FKL (19.0) is nearly 50% higher than SBIRR's (13.4), correctly reflecting that its dynamics however well it matches snapshots are fundamentally different from the ground truth. The marginal metrics, operating only on static distributions at fixed times, are completely blind to this.

5 The Petal case: same snapshot, completely different dynamics

The Petal dataset has eight sinusoidal branches radiating from the origin cells start clustered together and bifurcate into distinct lineages. Getting this right means producing trajectories that fan out into the correct branches, not just landing near the right snapshot distributions.

At τ = 0.75, TIGON scores slightly better than MSBM on W₂ (0.155 vs 0.160) and clearly better on MMD (0.018 vs 0.057). Based on marginal metrics alone, you would declare TIGON the winner. FKL tells the opposite story: MSBM = 13.6, TIGON = 36.5. That's a 2.7× difference a massive gap invisible to W₂.

Trajectories converge to the snapshot positions but don't separate into branches along the way cells follow smooth undifferentiated paths. The snapshot is hit; the bifurcation is missed entirely.

This is the core problem with marginal evaluation in a single sentence: a method can get the destination right without getting the journey right. In biology, the journey which cells took which lineage, when the commitment happened, what the transition looked like is usually what you actually care about.

6 Real data: no ground truth, but FKL still reveals structure

On real scRNA-seq datasets there are no ground-truth trajectories cells are measured destructively, so individual paths can never be observed. Most evaluation protocols resort to marginal metrics for exactly this reason.

To apply FKL, the paper uses SBIRR as a reference: it explicitly models differentiation as a stochastic drift-diffusion process, which matches established biological understanding of how cells commit to lineages. FKL then quantifies how closely each other method recovers those stochastic dynamics. This is a principled proxy, not a ground truth but it captures something marginal metrics cannot: whether a method's implied dynamics are plausible.

Embryoid Body (EB)

3 training + 2 validation snapshots · 300 cells/snapshot · 5D PCA

FKL correctly recovers the similarity between SB-family methods: vSB, MSBM, and MFL all share the same core stochastic assumptions as the SBIRR reference, so their dynamics are similar and FKL reflects this.

Marginal metrics on EB are relatively consistent here SB methods score well on both marginals and FKL. This is the "easy" case where both types of evaluation happen to agree.

Human Embryonic Stem Cell (hESC)

3 training + 2 validation snapshots · 92–172 cells/snapshot · 5D PCA

This is where marginal metrics and FKL openly contradict each other. TIGON ranks best at the first validation timepoint under every single marginal metric (W₁, W₂, SWD, MWD, MMD). FKL disagrees sharply.

vSB FKL = 89.8 best
MSBM FKL = 111.6
TIGON FKL = 103.0
The gap: TIGON's ODE-based trajectories are too smooth they don't reflect the inherent stochasticity of stem cell differentiation. Marginals look fine; the dynamics are wrong. A researcher relying only on W₂ would conclude TIGON is the best available method for this dataset. FKL says otherwise.

7 What this means in practice

Three patterns emerge consistently across all five datasets:

Pattern What it means Example
Deterministic methods inflate their marginal scores ODE-based methods (TIGON) produce smooth paths that land near snapshot dots but miss stochastic structure. Marginals reward landing; FKL rewards the full path. hESC: TIGON ranks #1 on W₂, ~middle on FKL
SB methods without structural constraints can be deceptive vSB and MSBM connect point clouds without controlling intermediate dynamics. They score well at specific snapshots while generating implausible in-between trajectories. Repressilator: vSB competitive marginals, FKL = 32 vs SBIRR FKL = 13
Structural constraints in SBIRR pay off under FKL SBIRR consistently ranks #1 on FKL for the stochastic datasets. Its iterative reference refinement encodes knowledge about the dynamics and FKL measures exactly whether that knowledge is right. Best FKL on Lotka-Volterra (30.8), Repressilator (13.4)
The bottom line: marginal metrics measure whether a method can reconstruct static distributions at fixed timepoints. FKL measures whether a method has learned the actual dynamics of the system. These are different questions and for trajectory inference, the second question is the one that matters.
