Results

Standard metrics disagree. FKL doesn't.

Six trajectory inference methods are benchmarked on three synthetic datasets and two real single-cell datasets. Five widely-used marginal metrics produce inconsistent, contradictory rankings. FKL provides a single coherent ordering that matches visual inspection every time.

1 — The benchmark

Six methods spanning the main families of trajectory inference:

  • SBIRR — Schrödinger Bridge with iterative reference refinement. Injects structural constraints on the drift.
  • vSB — vanilla Schrödinger Bridge baseline, Brownian motion reference.
  • MSBM — Multi-Marginal Schrödinger Bridge Matching.
  • MFL — Mean-Field Langevin in path space. Targets drift-diffusion population dynamics.
  • AM — Action Matching. Learns deterministic velocity fields with optional stochastic variant.
  • TIGON — ODE-based. Formulates TI as dynamic optimal transport solved via neural ODEs.

Three synthetic datasets where ground-truth trajectories are known: Lotka-Volterra (predator-prey SDEs), Repressilator (three-gene oscillator), and Petal (branching differentiation). Plus two real scRNA-seq datasets: Embryoid Body (EB) and Human Embryonic Stem Cell (hESC) — where no ground truth exists.

Five marginal metrics evaluated at held-out timepoints: Earth Mover's Distance (W₁), Wasserstein-2 (W₂), Sliced Wasserstein (SWD), Max-Sliced Wasserstein (MWD), and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Plus FKL — the forward and reverse KL between full trajectory distributions.

3 — FKL gives a coherent ranking

FKL produces stable rankings that align with what you see when you actually look at the trajectories. Lower FKL = closer to the ground-truth path distribution. The validation set (VAL) — resampled from the same process — sets the baseline.

4 — The Petal case: same snapshot, different dynamics

The Petal dataset exposes the problem most clearly. At τ = 0.75, TIGON scores slightly better than MSBM on W₂ (0.155 vs 0.160) and clearly better on MMD (0.018 vs 0.057). Yet their trajectories are completely different.

MSBM (FKL = 13.6) correctly captures the branching structure of the Petal dataset — trajectories spread into distinct lineages. TIGON (FKL = 36.5) produces smooth, undifferentiated paths that converge to the same snapshot point but miss the bifurcation entirely. W₂ ranked TIGON as the winner. FKL correctly identifies MSBM.

5 — Real data: no ground truth, but FKL still reveals structure

On real scRNA-seq data there are no ground-truth trajectories. FKL uses SBIRR — which explicitly models stochastic differentiation dynamics — as the reference. FKL then measures how closely each method recovers those dynamics.

Embryoid Body (EB)

vSB, MSBM, and MFL all score well under FKL — they share the same Schrödinger Bridge assumptions as the reference, so their dynamics are inherently similar. Marginal metrics agree here, making EB the easier case.

FKL correctly recovers the similarity in dynamics between SB-family methods, while ranking AM and TIGON as less faithful to the reference process.

Human Embryonic Stem Cell (hESC)

TIGON ranks best at the first validation timepoint under every marginal metric. But FKL tells a different story: TIGON's ODE-based trajectories are too smooth — they miss the stochasticity that characterises real differentiation dynamics.

FKL = 103 for TIGON vs FKL = 90 for vSB — a gap invisible to W₂, which scores TIGON as the clear winner on marginals.
The key pattern across all datasets: methods that produce deterministic or overly smooth trajectories (TIGON, AM) tend to score well on marginal metrics — cells land near the right dots — but score poorly on FKL, because the paths themselves don't reflect the stochastic nature of the process. FKL is the only metric that catches this.
