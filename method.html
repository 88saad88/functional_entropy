Method

Comparing distributions over trajectories

FKL measures the KL divergence between two probability measures over function space — the full space of possible trajectories. Here are the two conditions required, and the formula that makes it computable.

The object we want to measure

A trajectory inference method outputs a probability measure over paths. Call it \(\nu\). Two methods give us \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\). We want one number capturing how different these two distributions are across the entire space of trajectories. The natural choice is the KL divergence:

KL divergence on path space \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) = \int_H \log \frac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\; d\nu^A(x) \]

The term \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\) is the Radon–Nikodym derivative — the density of \(\nu^A\) with respect to \(\nu^B\). For each trajectory \(x\), it answers: how much more likely is this path under \(\nu^A\) than under \(\nu^B\)? When the two distributions agree everywhere, this ratio is 1 and KL = 0. When they diverge, KL grows.

The catch: in infinite-dimensional spaces the Radon–Nikodym derivative is extremely easy to break. Without the right setup it doesn't exist and \(\mathrm{KL} = +\infty\) trivially. Two assumptions fix this.

Two assumptions that make it well-defined

Assumption 2.1

Absolute continuity — \(\nu^A \ll \nu^B\), with bounded Radon–Nikodym derivative

We require \(\nu^A\) to be absolutely continuous with respect to \(\nu^B\): every set of trajectories that has zero probability under \(\nu^B\) must also have zero probability under \(\nu^A\). Without this, \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}\) does not exist and \(\mathrm{KL} = +\infty\) trivially. We additionally require the derivative to be bounded — \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B} \le M\) almost surely — so the log term inside the integral stays finite.

Concretely, this means \(\nu^A\) cannot assign positive probability to a region of trajectory space where \(\nu^B\) has assigned exactly zero. A method that generates trajectories in a completely different region of path space than the reference would violate this. In practice, since both methods are trained on the same snapshot data and share the same noise structure, this is readily satisfied — it can always be enforced by pre-processing with a deterministic smoothing map \(T: H \to H_C\).

Assumption 2.2

Cameron–Martin support — \(\nu^A(H_C) = \nu^B(H_C) = 1\)

The noise measure is a Gaussian \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, C)\) on the Hilbert space \(H\), where \(C\) is a covariance operator assumed to be trace-class, self-adjoint, bounded, and strictly positive. Trace-class means \(\sum_k \lambda_k < \infty\) for its eigenvalues — this ensures the Gaussian is a proper probability measure on \(H\) and not just formal.

In infinite dimensions, two Gaussian measures with the same covariance are either equivalent or mutually singular depending entirely on where their means live. The Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\) is the subspace of functions reachable by \(C\): formally \(H_C = C^{1/2}(H)\), equipped with norm \(\|h\|_{H_C}^2 = \|C^{-1/2}h\|_H^2\). The Cameron–Martin theorem states that shifting a Gaussian by \(h\) produces an equivalent measure if and only if \(h \in H_C\) — this is one key condition, not the only one, for absolute continuity between Gaussians.

Requiring \(\nu^A(H_C) = \nu^B(H_C) = 1\) ensures both trajectory distributions are supported on this smooth subspace, guaranteeing a common dominating measure and making the Radon–Nikodym derivative computable via the Cameron–Martin theorem. In practice this is ensured by using a trace-class noise covariance — concretely, a Matérn kernel with \(\nu = 0.5\) — which confines sampled functions to \(H_C\) by construction, unlike white noise which lives outside it.

Together these two assumptions guarantee the KL is finite and the Radon–Nikodym derivative exists. They also unlock the Cameron–Martin theorem, which gives an explicit formula for density ratios under shifts in function space — and that formula is the key step in deriving the tractable FKL estimator.

From density ratios to velocity fields via FFM

Rather than estimating the Radon–Nikodym derivative directly — which is hard — FKL re-expresses the KL in terms of velocity fields, using Functional Flow Matching (FFM).

For each method, construct a linear interpolation between a noise sample \(X_0 \sim \mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0,C)\) and a trajectory sample \(X_1 \sim \nu\):

Linear interpolation in function space \[ X_t = (1-t)\,X_0 + t\,X_1, \qquad t \in [0,1] \]

This sweeps a path of measures \(\mu_t = \mathrm{Law}(X_t)\) from \(\mu_0\) at \(t=0\) to \(\nu\) at \(t=1\). The key identity links the velocity field to a conditional expectation:

Velocity field via conditional expectation \[ v_t(x) = \mathbb{E}[X_1 - X_0 \mid X_t = x] = \frac{1}{1-t}\,\mathbb{E}[X_1 \mid X_t = x] - \frac{x}{1-t} \]

A neural network learns \(v_t\) by regressing \(X_1 - X_0\) given \(X_t\) — no ODE simulation needed during training or estimation. The crucial insight: since \(r_0 = \tfrac{d\mu^A_0}{d\mu^B_0} = 1\) everywhere (both start from the same noise \(\mu_0\)), the KL at \(t=0\) is zero. All the divergence accumulates as \(t\) grows and the two flows separate. The continuity equation then lets us write:

KL via the continuity equation \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) = \int_0^1 \int_H \bigl\langle v^A_t(x) - v^B_t(x),\, \nabla_x \log r_t(x) \bigr\rangle \, d\mu^A_t(x)\, dt \]

Substituting the Cameron–Martin expression for \(\nabla_x \log r_t\) yields the final closed form.

The key formula

Functional KL — main result (Eq. 9) \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) \;=\; \int_0^1 \!\int_H \frac{t}{1-t} \;\bigl\|v_t^A(x) - v_t^B(x)\bigr\|_{C^{1/2}H}^{\,2} \;d\mu^A_t(x)\;dt \]

Why this is powerful: the KL divergence between two infinite-dimensional trajectory distributions reduces to the expected squared difference of two learned functions. No density estimation, no ODE integration — just two neural-network forward passes per sample.

Estimation in three steps

1

Train two velocity networks

One network \(v^A_\theta\) on samples from \(\nu^A\), one \(v^B_\theta\) on samples from \(\nu^B\). Both use a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) architecture — it operates natively on functions, independently of discretisation resolution, which is why the estimator stays stable under upsampling.

2

Sample interpolated trajectories

Draw \(x_1 \sim \nu^A\), \(x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C)\), \(t \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]\). Form \(x_t = tx_1 + (1-t)x_0\). This gives a sample distributed as \(\mu^A_t\) — the interpolation measure at time \(t\) for method A.

3

Average the integrand

Evaluate \(\tfrac{t}{1-t}\|v^A_\theta(x_t) - v^B_\theta(x_t)\|^2_{C^{1/2}H}\) and average over many such samples. This is a Monte Carlo estimate of the double integral — no ODE simulation, no density estimation, just forward passes through the two trained networks.
