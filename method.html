Method

Comparing distributions over trajectories

FKL measures the KL divergence between two probability measures over function space — the full space of possible trajectories. Here's the idea, the two conditions required, and the formula that makes it computable.

The object we want to measure

A trajectory inference method doesn't output a single path — it outputs a distribution over paths. Call it \(\nu\). Two methods give us \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\). We want one number that captures how different these two distributions are, across the entire space of possible trajectories.

The natural choice is the KL divergence:

KL divergence on path space \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) = \int \log \frac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\; d\nu^A(x) \]

The term \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\) is the density ratio: for each trajectory \(x\), how much more likely is it under \(\nu^A\) than under \(\nu^B\)? When the two distributions agree, this ratio is 1 everywhere and KL = 0. When they diverge, KL grows.

The catch: in infinite-dimensional spaces this density ratio is extremely easy to break. Without the right setup, it doesn't exist and \(\mathrm{KL} = +\infty\) trivially. Two conditions fix this.

Two conditions that make it well-defined

Assumption 1

Absolute continuity — \(\nu^A \ll \nu^B\)

Every set of trajectories that is impossible under \(\nu^B\) must also be impossible under \(\nu^A\). If \(\nu^A\) places mass somewhere \(\nu^B\) has ruled out entirely, the density ratio doesn't exist and KL blows up.

In practice this is a mild condition: it says the two methods are exploring roughly the same territory in trajectory space, just with different weights.

Assumption 2

Cameron–Martin support — trajectories must be smooth enough

In infinite dimensions, two Gaussian measures are almost always mutually singular — they live on completely disjoint sets, making any density ratio undefined. The only exception is when their means differ by a vector in the Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\): the subspace of functions reachable by the covariance operator \(C\).

Requiring both \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\) to be supported on \(H_C\) ensures they share enough common structure for the density ratio to exist. In practice this means using a smooth reference noise (Matérn covariance) rather than white noise — which also happens to produce more realistic trajectory priors.

Together these two conditions guarantee the KL is finite and the density ratio exists. They also unlock the Cameron–Martin theorem, which gives an explicit formula for how density ratios shift when you move in function space — and that formula is what leads to the tractable estimator below.

From density ratios to velocity fields

Rather than estimating the density ratio directly — which is hard — FKL re-expresses the KL in terms of velocity fields.

The idea: for each method, build a continuous flow that transports a reference Gaussian \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, C)\) toward the method's trajectory distribution \(\nu\). This flow is parameterised by a velocity field \(v_t(x)\) — a function that, at each time \(t\) and location \(x\) in function space, says which direction the distribution is moving. The velocity field is learned by a neural network.

The simulation below lets you see what this looks like. Each arrow shows the velocity at a grid point. Method A (purple) drifts smoothly; Method B (amber) oscillates. At the same location \(x\) and time \(t\), the two arrows point in different directions — and that difference is exactly what FKL measures.

The key formula

Combining the Cameron–Martin theorem with the continuity equation for the flow, the paper derives a closed-form expression for FKL purely in terms of velocity fields:

Functional KL — main result (Eq. 9) \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) \;=\; \int_0^1 \!\int_H \frac{t}{1-t} \;\bigl\|v_t^A(x) - v_t^B(x)\bigr\|_{C^{1/2}H}^{\,2} \;d\mu^A_t(x)\;dt \]

Squared norm in the Cameron–Martin space — this is where Assumption 2 enters. It weights disagreements by the geometry of the reference noise covariance.

A time weight that upweights discrepancies near t = 1, where trajectory distributions have had the most time to diverge. It blows up at t = 1, which is why importance sampling is used in practice.
dμAt dt
We integrate over trajectories drawn from method A's interpolation path at each time t. The estimator is computed entirely from samples of νA — no samples from νB needed at evaluation time.
Why this is powerful: the KL divergence between two infinite-dimensional trajectory distributions reduces to the expected squared difference of two functions. No density estimation, no ODE simulation — just two neural-network forward passes per sample.

Estimation in three steps

1

Train two velocity networks

One network \(v^A_\theta\) on samples from method A, one \(v^B_\theta\) on method B. Both use a Fourier Neural Operator architecture so they operate on functions directly, resolution-independently.

2

Sample interpolated trajectories

Draw \(x_1 \sim \nu^A\), \(x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C)\), \(t \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]\). Form \(x_t = t x_1 + (1-t) x_0\). This gives a point on the interpolation path at time \(t\), distributed as \(\mu^A_t\).

3

Average the integrand

Evaluate \(\tfrac{t}{1-t}\|v^A_\theta(x_t) - v^B_\theta(x_t)\|^2\) and average over many samples. No ODE integration required — just forward passes through the two trained networks.
