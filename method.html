FKL · Path-Space Divergence Motivation Method Results
Method

Measuring the distance between two stories about the same cells

FKL estimates the KL divergence between two probability measures on function space — the space of all possible trajectories. Getting there requires three ingredients: the right mathematical setting, two technical assumptions, and a clever reformulation that replaces an intractable integral with something a neural network can learn.

1 — What we are trying to measure

A trajectory inference method outputs a probability measure over paths — not a single trajectory, but a full distribution \(\nu\) over the space of all possible cellular journeys \(H\). Two methods give us two such measures, \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\). We want a single number that says how different they are.

The natural choice is the Kullback–Leibler divergence:

Definition — KL divergence on function space \[ \mathrm{KL}\!\left(\nu^A \,\|\, \nu^B\right) \;=\; \int_{H} \log \frac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\; d\nu^A(x) \]

Here \(H\) is a real separable Hilbert space — think of it as the infinite-dimensional space whose points are entire time-series (one point = one possible trajectory of a cell). The Radon–Nikodym derivative \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}\) is the density of \(\nu^A\) relative to \(\nu^B\): it tells you, for each trajectory \(x\), how much more (or less) likely it is under \(\nu^A\) than under \(\nu^B\).

Why KL and not Wasserstein? Wasserstein distances on function space are notoriously hard to estimate. KL, while also non-trivial, admits a clever reformulation via velocity fields that makes it tractable — as we will see in Section 3.

2 — Two assumptions that make the math work

The KL divergence on infinite-dimensional spaces is extremely easy to break — it is \(+\infty\) the moment the two measures don't "live in the same region." Two assumptions prevent this.

Assumption 2.1 — Absolute continuity

\(\nu^A \ll \nu^B\), with bounded Radon–Nikodym derivative

We require that \(\nu^A\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\nu^B\): every set of trajectories that has zero probability under \(\nu^B\) also has zero probability under \(\nu^A\). In other words, \(\nu^A\) cannot place mass in a region that \(\nu^B\) has completely ruled out. Without this, \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}\) does not exist and \(\mathrm{KL} = +\infty\) trivially.

We additionally require \(\tfrac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B} \le M\) almost surely — the density is bounded — so that the log term inside the integral stays finite and the estimator doesn't blow up.

Assumption 2.2 — Cameron–Martin support

Both measures live inside the Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\)

In infinite dimensions, Gaussian measures are mutually singular unless their means differ by a vector in a very specific subspace — the Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\). Think of \(H_C\) as the "smooth" directions of the reference Gaussian \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, C)\): it consists of all functions reachable by the covariance operator \(C\), and has the norm \(\|h\|_{H_C}^2 = \langle C^{-1}h, h\rangle\).

The assumption \(\nu^A(H_C) = \nu^B(H_C) = 1\) ensures both trajectory distributions are supported on this smooth subspace. This is what makes the Radon–Nikodym derivative well-defined and computable via the Cameron–Martin theorem — a classical result that gives an explicit formula for density ratios when you shift a Gaussian by a Cameron–Martin vector.

In practice this is satisfied by smoothing trajectories before fitting — applying a deterministic map \(T: H \to H_C\) via, e.g., a Matérn kernel. This is why the paper uses trace-class noise covariances (Matérn with \(\nu = 0.5\)) rather than white noise: they produce functions that land in \(H_C\) by construction.

Together these two assumptions guarantee: (i) the KL is finite, (ii) the density ratio exists, and (iii) we can use the Cameron–Martin theorem to relate density ratios to velocity fields — which is the key step.

3 — Interpolating between distributions via velocity fields

Rather than working with \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\) directly, the paper uses Functional Flow Matching (FFM) to represent each measure through a flow. The idea: build a path of intermediate measures interpolating from a reference Gaussian \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0,C)\) to the target measure \(\nu\).

Concretely, given \(X_0 \sim \mu_0\) and \(X_1 \sim \nu\), define:

Linear interpolation in function space \[ X_t \;=\; (1-t)\,X_0 \;+\; t\,X_1, \qquad t \in [0,1] \]

The induced law \(\mu_t = \text{Law}(X_t)\) sweeps from \(\mu_0\) at \(t=0\) to \(\nu\) at \(t=1\). This path of measures is generated by a velocity field \(v_t : H \to H\) satisfying the continuity equation. The key identity (from the conditional expectation structure of the interpolation) is:

Velocity field — conditional expectation form \[ v_t(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[X_1 - X_0 \;\big|\; X_t = x\right] \;=\; \frac{1}{1-t}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[X_1 \;\big|\; X_t = x\right] - \frac{x}{1-t} \]

This velocity field is what a neural network learns during FFM training. For a given measure \(\nu\), you train a network \(v_\theta\) to predict \(X_1 - X_0\) given \(X_t\) — a regression problem on function space. 4 — The FKL formula

With both velocity fields in hand, the paper's central result expresses the KL divergence in a closed, computable form. Starting from the continuity equation and the Cameron–Martin formula for density ratios, after careful manipulation (see paper §3) one arrives at:

Main result — Functional KL (Equation 9) \[ \mathrm{KL}\!\left(\nu^A \,\|\, \nu^B\right) \;=\; \int_0^1 \int_{H} \frac{t}{1-t} \;\bigl\|v_t^A(x) - v_t^B(x)\bigr\|_{C^{1/2} H}^{\,2} \;d\mu^A_t(x)\;dt \]

Let's unpack each piece:

  • \(v_t^A(x) - v_t^B(x)\) — the difference between the two velocity fields at point \(x\) and time \(t\). If both methods agree on how trajectories flow at this point, this is zero.
  • \(\|\cdot\|_{C^{1/2}H}^2\) — the squared norm in the Cameron–Martin space, weighted by the covariance \(C\). This is what ties Assumption 2.2 to the formula: the norm is only finite because both fields live in \(H_C\).
  • \(\tfrac{t}{1-t}\) — a time-weighting factor that upweights discrepancies near \(t=1\) (where the trajectories have diverged most) and downweights early times. Note it blows up at \(t=1\), which is why importance sampling is used in practice.
  • \(d\mu^A_t(x)\,dt\) — the integral is over \(\nu^A\)-distributed trajectories at each interpolation time \(t\). This means we sample paths from method A and evaluate the integrand along them.
Why this is remarkable: the KL divergence between two infinite-dimensional probability measures reduces to the expected squared difference of two vector-valued functions — something estimable purely from samples, with no ODE integration required.

5 — Estimation in practice

The formula above is still an integral over an infinite-dimensional space. Making it computable requires three steps.

1

Project onto a finite basis

Trajectories are projected onto the first \(K\) modes of an orthonormal basis \(\{e_k\}\) (e.g. Fourier modes). The KL on projected measures \(\hat{\nu}^A_K, \hat{\nu}^B_K\) converges to the true FKL as \(K \to \infty\) by the martingale convergence theorem.

2

Learn velocity fields with a Fourier Neural Operator

Two networks \(v^A_\theta\) and \(v^B_\theta\) are trained independently on samples from \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\) using the FFM loss. A Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) is used as the architecture — it operates natively on functions (discretisation-independent) and is equivariant to resolution changes, which is why the estimator remains stable under upsampling.

3

Monte Carlo integration

Sample \(x_1^A \sim \nu^A\), \(x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C)\), \(t \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]\). Construct \(x_t^A = t x_1^A + (1-t)x_0\). Evaluate \(\frac{t}{1-t}\|v^A_\theta(x_t^A) - v^B_\theta(x_t^A)\|^2_{C^{1/2}H}\). Average over many samples. No simulation of dynamics required — just forward passes through two networks.

Cost: training two neural networks (paid once). Inference: a handful of forward passes per estimate. This scales to realistic numbers of snapshots and trajectory resolutions — which is why it works on real single-cell data.
