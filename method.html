Method

Comparing distributions over trajectories

FKL measures the KL divergence between two probability measures over function space — the full space of possible trajectories. Here's the idea, the two conditions required, and the formula that makes it computable.

The object we want to measure

A trajectory inference method doesn't output a single path — it outputs a probability distribution over paths. Call it \(\nu\). Two methods give us \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\). We want one number that captures how different these two distributions are across the entire space of possible trajectories.

The natural choice is the Kullback–Leibler divergence:

KL divergence on path space \[ \mathrm{KL}(\nu^A \| \nu^B) \;=\; \int_H \log \frac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\; d\nu^A(x) \]

The term \(\frac{d\nu^A}{d\nu^B}(x)\) is the Radon–Nikodym derivative (also called the density ratio): for each trajectory \(x\), it says how much more probable that trajectory is under \(\nu^A\) than under \(\nu^B\). When both distributions agree everywhere this ratio is 1 and KL = 0. When they differ, KL grows.

The difficulty is that in infinite-dimensional spaces — where trajectories live — the Radon–Nikodym derivative is fragile: without structural conditions it doesn't exist and \(\mathrm{KL} = +\infty\) trivially. Two assumptions fix this.

Two conditions that make it well-defined

Assumption 1

Absolute continuity — \(\nu^A \ll \nu^B\)

Formally: every set of trajectories with probability zero under \(\nu^B\) must also have probability zero under \(\nu^A\). If \(\nu^A\) places mass somewhere \(\nu^B\) has ruled out entirely, the density ratio is \(\frac{\text{positive}}{0}\) — undefined — and the integral blows up.

What this means concretely: it requires that both methods share the same support in trajectory space. Any trajectory that is possible under one method must have a non-zero probability under the other — not necessarily the same probability, but positive. In other words, neither method should generate trajectories that are structurally impossible for the other.

How to ensure this in practice: the paper additionally requires a uniform bound \(d\nu^A/d\nu^B \leq M\) almost surely. This is automatically satisfied when both methods are trained under the same noise model and data distribution, as the density ratio is continuous. The bound ensures the KL integral is not just well-defined but finite.

Assumption 2

Cameron–Martin support — trajectories must lie in \(H_C\)

This assumption is specific to infinite dimensions, and understanding it requires knowing what goes wrong with Gaussian measures on function spaces.

Finite dimensions — absolutely continuous
\(\mathcal{N}(m_1,\Sigma)\) and \(\mathcal{N}(m_2,\Sigma)\) share overlapping support. The Radon–Nikodym derivative exists. KL = \(\tfrac{1}{2}\|m_1-m_2\|^2_{\Sigma^{-1}}\).
Infinite dimensions — mutually singular (generically)
Same setup on function space. The two measures live on disjoint sets with probability 1. No Radon–Nikodym derivative. KL = +∞.

The Cameron–Martin theorem gives the precise condition under which two Gaussian measures on a Hilbert space avoid mutual singularity. Two measures \(\mathcal{N}(m_1, C)\) and \(\mathcal{N}(m_2, C)\) are mutually absolutely continuous — and have a well-defined density ratio — if and only if \(m_1 - m_2\) lies in the Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\). Note that this is not the only setting where two measures can be absolutely continuous — it is the characterisation specific to Gaussian measures with equal covariance \(C\), which is the case relevant here.

The Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\) is the reproducing kernel Hilbert space of \(C\): the set of functions of the form \(C^{1/2}h\) for \(h\) in the base Hilbert space. It can be thought of as the set of "perturbation directions" that the Gaussian measure can absorb without becoming singular. The richer \(H_C\) is, the easier the condition is to satisfy.

This depends critically on the covariance operator \(C\):

  • White noise covariance (all frequencies equal weight): \(H_C\) is very small — only genuinely smoother-than-generic functions qualify. Most pairs of trajectory distributions give mutually singular Gaussians.
  • Matérn covariance \(C = \sigma^2(-\Delta + \tau^2 I)^{-\alpha}\) (decaying for high frequencies): \(H_C\) consists of Sobolev \(H^\alpha\) functions — a much larger and practically relevant space. Any piecewise-smooth trajectory automatically satisfies the condition.
Assumption 2 — formal

Both \(\nu^A\) and \(\nu^B\) are supported on \(H_{\mu_0}\)

The noise \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, C)\) is fixed. Both trajectory distributions must assign probability 1 to the Cameron–Martin space \(H_C\) of this noise. Under this condition, the interpolated measures \(\mu_t^A\) and \(\mu_t^B\) remain mutually absolutely continuous for all \(t \in [0,1)\), and the density ratio \(d\mu_t^A / d\mu_t^B\) exists at every \(t\).

How to ensure this in practice: apply a deterministic smoothing projection \(T : H \to H_C\) to all trajectory data before training. Concretely, this means (a) projecting onto a finite Fourier basis (the paper uses 32–64 modes), and (b) using a Matérn(ν=0.5) covariance for \(\mu_0\), whose Cameron–Martin space is \(H^1\) (once-weakly-differentiable functions). Any \(L^2\) trajectory that is piecewise smooth will lie in \(H^1\) after such projection.

Note: the condition can be relaxed — it suffices for one of the two measures to be fully supported on \(H_C\), when combined with Assumption 1's bounded density ratio. The paper uses both for cleaner theory.

Together these two conditions ensure the KL is finite and the Radon–Nikodym derivative exists. They also unlock the Cameron–Martin theorem's explicit formula for the log-gradient of the density ratio — which is the key ingredient in turning the KL integral into something computable from velocity fields.

Functional Flow Matching: transporting noise to trajectories

Rather than estimating the density ratio directly, FKL routes through a continuous interpolation from a tractable noise distribution to the trajectory distribution of interest.

The construction is Functional Flow Matching (FFM). Fix a noise measure \(\mu_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, C)\) with a Matérn covariance \(C\). For a trajectory distribution \(\nu\), define the linear interpolation:

FFM linear interpolation \[ X_t \;=\; (1-t)\,X_0 \;+\; t\,X_1, \qquad X_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C),\quad X_1 \sim \nu,\quad t\in[0,1] \]

This defines a path of measures \(\mu_t\) from pure noise (\(t=0\)) to the trajectory distribution (\(t=1\)). At \(t=0\): both methods start from identical noise samples — no information about which method you're using. As \(t\to 1\): Method A (purple) converges to smooth drift trajectories; Method B (amber) to oscillatory ones. FKL quantifies the divergence between these two paths of measures.

The flow is governed by a velocity field \(v_t : H \to H\) satisfying the continuity equation \(\partial_t \mu_t + \nabla \cdot (\mu_t v_t) = 0\). Under the linear interpolation, the conditional velocity field has a closed form:

Conditional velocity (FFM) \[ v_t(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{1-t}\,\mathbb{E}[X_1 - X_0 \mid X_t = x] \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[X_1 \mid X_t = x] - x}{1-t} \]

This is approximated by a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO), an architecture that processes functions at any resolution by operating in frequency space — making the velocity estimator resolution-independent.

From the Radon–Nikodym derivative to velocity fields

For two methods A and B, both starting from the same noise \(\mu_0\), define the density ratio at interpolation time \(t\): \(r_t = d\mu_t^A / d\mu_t^B\). At \(t=0\), \(r_0 \equiv 1\) (both distributions are identical). At \(t=1\), \(r_1 = d\nu^A / d\nu^B\) is exactly the density ratio we need. The KL is \(\int \log r_1\, d\nu^A\).

The derivation uses the structure of the interpolation — specifically, that \(r_t^K = \mathbb{E}^B[r_t | \mathcal{G}_K]\) is a martingale as a function of the truncation dimension \(K\) — together with the continuity equation, to express the KL as an integral over \(t\):

①
Write KL as accumulation over \(t\). Since \(r_0 \equiv 1\), the KL equals the total change in \(\int \log r_t^K\, d\mu_t^A\) from \(t=0\) to \(t=1\), which decomposes into a time integral via the continuity equation.
②
Apply the continuity equation to \(\log r_t\). The time derivative yields \(\int \langle v_t^A - v_t^B,\, \nabla_x \log r_t \rangle\, d\mu_t^A\).
③
Use the Cameron–Martin formula for \(\nabla_x \log r_t\). By Assumptions 1–2 and the Cameron–Martin theorem: \(\nabla_x \log \frac{d\mu_t^{A,B}}{d\rho_t}(x) = \frac{t}{(1-t)^2} C^{-1}\mathbb{E}[X_1 | X_t = x]\). This links the log-gradient of the density ratio directly to the velocity field (Eq. 8).
④
Substitute, simplify, take \(K \to \infty\). After substituting and applying the Martingale convergence theorem, all terms involving individual density ratios cancel, leaving only the squared velocity difference — the clean formula below.

The key formula

The squared Cameron–Martin norm — the RKHS norm of \(C\). It down-weights high-frequency discrepancies (outside \(H_C\)) and up-weights low-frequency ones that the noise model can represent. This is where Assumption 2 enters.
t / (1−t)
A time weight that grows toward \(t=1\). Near \(t=0\) both distributions look the same (pure noise), so discrepancies there matter less. Near \(t=1\) disagreements reflect genuine differences in the trajectory distributions. The divergence at \(t=1\) is handled by importance sampling in practice.

We integrate over interpolated points \(x_t \sim \mu_t^A\) at each \(t\). Both velocity networks are evaluated at the same point — no samples from \(\nu^B\) needed at estimation time.
Why this matters: the KL divergence between two infinite-dimensional trajectory distributions reduces to the expected squared difference of two velocity functions, weighted by \(t/(1-t)\). No density estimation in high dimensions, no ODE integration — just two FNO forward passes per sample.

Estimation in three steps

1

Train two velocity networks on trajectory samples

One FNO network \(v^A_\theta\) on sampled trajectories from method A, one \(v^B_\theta\) from method B. The training loss is the FFM objective: \(\mathbb{E}\|\hat{v}_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0)\|^2\) over interpolated samples. In practice the loss is a mixture \(w\,\mathcal{L}_\text{FFM} + (1-w)\,\mathcal{L}_\text{FKL}\) with \(w\) decayed from 1 to 0 during training, which jointly optimises generation quality and KL accuracy.

2

Sample interpolated trajectory points

Draw \(x_1 \sim \nu^A\), \(x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,C)\), \(t \sim p(t)\) (importance-sampled to handle the \(t/(1-t)\) divergence). Form \(x_t = t\,x_1 + (1-t)\,x_0 \sim \mu_t^A\).

3

Average the integrand over samples

Evaluate \(\tfrac{t}{1-t}\,\|v^A_\theta(x_t,t) - v^B_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2_{C^{1/2}H}\) and average. Both networks are evaluated at the same interpolated point \(x_t\). No ODE integration is required.
