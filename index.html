You can't judge a trajectory by its snapshots

In single-cell biology, we never see how individual cells move — only frozen cross-sections of a population at discrete moments. Most methods for reconstructing cell trajectories are evaluated on how well they reproduce those snapshots. But two methods can match every snapshot perfectly while telling completely opposite stories about the underlying dynamics.

This is the problem FKL — functional KL divergence — was built to fix.

This is the gap FKL closes. Marginal metrics only ask "do simulated cells land near the observed dots?" — never "did they travel the right path to get there?" FKL operates directly on path space, so it can tell these stories apart.

Why this matters

In single-cell genomics, trajectories encode fate decisions — which stem cells become which cell types, and when. Getting the dynamics wrong can lead to incorrect biological conclusions even if every snapshot looks right.

Current benchmarks in the field evaluate methods by their ability to predict held-out marginals. This paper shows that five widely-used metrics can produce inconsistent and sometimes contradictory rankings of the same methods — and that FKL resolves those contradictions by operating directly on path space.
