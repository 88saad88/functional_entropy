Same snapshots. Completely different stories.

In single-cell biology, measuring a cell destroys it. All we ever see are frozen cross-sections — independent samples at a handful of timepoints. The standard way to evaluate trajectory inference methods is to check how well they reproduce those snapshots. But that test is blind to something fundamental: how the cells actually moved.

FKL — functional KL divergence — is the first practical metric that operates on full trajectory distributions rather than marginals.

The non-identifiability problem. A set of time-indexed marginals can be realised by infinitely many path measures. Matching snapshots well tells you nothing about which of those path measures a method has found — or whether it found a plausible one at all.

Why this matters for biology

Trajectories in single-cell data encode fate decisions — which stem cells commit to which lineages, and when branching happens. A method that gets the snapshots right but the dynamics wrong can lead to incorrect biological conclusions: a smooth drift where there should be a sharp bifurcation, or oscillations where there should be steady progression.

This paper benchmarks six state-of-the-art trajectory inference methods using five standard marginal metrics. The metrics produce inconsistent and contradictory rankings of the same methods across timepoints and datasets. FKL resolves those contradictions by measuring what actually matters — the distance between full trajectory distributions.
